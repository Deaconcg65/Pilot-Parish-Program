Before We Begin

Presence Is Not Strategy

An Honest Question
"We have a website. We publish a bulletin every week. We make announcements at Mass. We have a sign out front. What else do we need?"

This is the most common answer a parish gives when asked about communications. And it is a completely understandable one. It is also, with respect, a description of the bare minimum required to prove you exist — not a plan for reaching people, growing a community, or advancing the mission of the Church.

A website tells people you're open. A bulletin tells the people already there what's happening next week. An announcement at the ambo tells the people already in the pews what the people already in the pews need to know. These are infrastructure. They are necessary — and they are not enough.

Here is the honest distinction between presence and strategy:

Presence says… Strategy asks…
"We updated the website." Did anyone find us who wasn't already looking? What did they do when they got there?
"The bulletin went out." Did anyone act on it? What was the one thing we wanted someone to do — and did we make that clear?
"We made the announcement." Was that announcement designed for the people in the room, or for the people we wish were in the room?
"We have social media." Are we building a relationship with people who haven't decided about us yet — or just narrating events to people who already know?
"We sent an email." Who received it? Did they open it? Was it written for them — or written because it was someone's job to send it?

The good news: most parishes are only a framework away from making what they already do far more effective. This document provides that framework.

The Foundation

What Communications Is Actually For

Every bulletin, every post, every email, every page of your website either advances someone's journey toward deeper faith and fuller participation — or it doesn't. The goal of parish communications is to move people along that spectrum. Not to fill space. Not to check the box. To move people.

Most parish communications is built for people at steps 3, 4, and 5 — the people already there. The people at steps 1 and 2 rarely appear in a bulletin. And yet that is precisely where parish growth begins.

The single most important strategic shift any parish can make is to start asking, for every communication: who is this for, and what step are we trying to move them to?

Know Your Audience

Your Audience Is Not One Group

The most common failure in parish communications is writing for a generic "parishioner" who doesn't actually exist. Real parishioners are complex. They sit at different points in their faith journey. They are in different seasons of life. They experience and seek God differently. Effective communications accounts for all three dimensions.

1

Engagement Level — Where they are in their relationship with the parish

Are they weekly Mass-goers or Christmas-and-Easter? Have they drifted away? Are they looking but haven't walked in yet? Each group requires a different door, a different tone, a different invitation.

"How connected is this person right now?"
2

Life Stage — Who they are and what they're carrying

A young adult searching for community and a 68-year-old widow are both "active parishioners." They are not the same person. Their needs, their available time, their motivations, and the messages that will reach them are entirely different.

"What season of life is shaping what they need right now?"
3

Faith Experience Type — How they engage with God and community

Some people are drawn through intellectual formation. Others through devotion and beauty. Others through service and belonging. One message, one channel, one style doesn't reach all of them — and trying to reach all of them equally usually reaches none of them well.

"What draws this person deeper?"
Dimension One

Engagement Level — The Six Starting Points

These six groups represent where people currently are in their engagement with your parish. They're a starting point for understanding your audience — not a complete picture. The critical insight: most parish communications is unconsciously designed for the first two groups only. The people who most need to be reached are in the last four.

1

The Core Active

Weekly Mass, gives, volunteers, knows everyone
Communication Goal
Deepen engagement. Mobilize them as ambassadors. Equip them to invite others into what they already love.
Watch for: Over-communicating to this group at the expense of everyone else.
2

The Engaged Occasional

2–3x/month, connected but not fully committed
Communication Goal
Create one clear next step — a ministry, a small group, a giving enrollment. Remove the friction between interest and action.
Watch for: Assuming they know what you're offering. They often don't.
3

The Peripheral Believer

Christmas, Easter, sacraments, funerals — and that's mostly it
Communication Goal
Warm, low-pressure, non-guilt-driven invitations to entry points that don't require full commitment. Make coming back feel safe and easy.
Watch for: Guilt-based messaging. It accelerates drift, it doesn't reverse it.
4

The Lapsed Catholic

Former regular, knows the parish, has drifted — often with a reason
Communication Goal
Low-barrier re-entry that isn't Mass. Community events, service opportunities, relationships — not institutional calls to return.
Watch for: "Come back to church" messaging. They know where it is. That's not why they left.
5

The Seeker

New to the area, spiritually curious, exploring — not yet connected anywhere
Communication Goal
Answer the questions they're actually asking: Is this place welcoming? Do people like me come here? Will I be judged? Your website is their first conversation with you.
Watch for: Assuming they know anything about Catholic practice. They often don't — and that's not a problem, it's an opportunity.
6

The Digital Follower

Engages online, watches livestreams, rarely or never in person
Communication Goal
Build the relationship digitally first. Identify bridge moments — a Lenten series, a community meal, a service project — that feel like natural reasons to show up in person.
Watch for: Ignoring them because they're not in the building. They're listening. Give them something worth hearing.
Dimension Two

Life Stage — Who They Actually Are

Engagement level tells you where someone is. Life stage tells you who they are and what they're carrying. Two "engaged occasional" parishioners might need completely different messages, channels, and entry points based solely on where they are in life. Communications that ignores this ends up feeling generic — because it is.

Young Adults
18–28, often unattached
What drives them

Belonging and authenticity. Faith as identity, not obligation. Community before doctrine. They can smell inauthenticity from a distance.

Reach them via

Social media, peer invitation, informal gatherings, young adult ministry with real social life attached

Young Families
Kids under 10, time-starved
What drives them

Faith formation for their children is the primary hook. They want a parish that makes it easy — for registration, for school, for sacramental prep.

Reach them via

School connections, simple online registration, email, clear website navigation to RE programs

Families with Teens
The drift window
What drives them

Youth ministry quality is everything. This is the most fragile family moment in parish life — teens pulling away, parents either doubling down or quietly checking out alongside them.

Reach them via

Youth ministry programming, service opportunities, parent engagement events, clear communication about teen programs

Empty Nesters
50s–60s, available, capable
What drives them

Meaning and service. Often the most available and financially capable segment in any parish — and the most underutilized. They want to contribute something real, not stuff envelopes.

Reach them via

Direct personal invitation to leadership and service roles, ministry fair presence, bulletin and email

Seniors
70+, active or isolated
What drives them

Faith as anchor, community as lifeline. Some are the most active people in the building. Others are increasingly homebound and at risk of total disconnection. Both need to feel seen.

Reach them via

Printed bulletin, phone calls, homebound ministry outreach, accessible website, in-person community events

In Transition
Widowed, divorced, grieving, newly arrived
What drives them

This is a specific pastoral moment that most parish communications never addresses. People in major life transitions either go deeper in faith — or disappear. Communications that meets them here is transformative.

Reach them via

Pastoral outreach, grief ministry, welcome programs, direct personal contact — not mass communications

Dimension Three

Faith Experience Type — How They Engage

Even among people at the same engagement level, in the same life stage, people draw near to God in different ways. A parish that only speaks one "language" of faith will only retain the people who happen to speak that language. Communications that acknowledges this variety — and programs that serve it — builds a more resilient community.

📖

The Intellectual

Wants to understand. Drawn by formation, rich homilies, study groups, and theological depth.

🕯️

The Devotional

Rosary, adoration, novenas. Drawn by beauty, tradition, reverence, and contemplative practice.

🤝

The Servant

Comes alive in action and community. Drawn by service opportunities, outreach, and belonging through doing.

✨

The Seeker

Less doctrinal, more experiential. Drawn by encounter, personal testimony, and felt moments of grace.

⛪

The Cultural Catholic

Identity-connected, not always practice-connected. Sacraments and feast days are anchors, not obligations.

Putting It Together

The Intersection Is Where Real Communication Lives

The Practical Reality

A Lapsed Catholic who is a 55-year-old empty nester and intellectually oriented needs an entirely different message than a Lapsed Catholic who is a 26-year-old young adult who drifted after college. Same box on the engagement chart. Completely different door.

You don't need to design a separate communication for every possible combination — that's not practical for any parish. But you do need to ask, for each major communication, who you are primarily writing for and whether it actually speaks to that person's real situation.

What This Looks Like in Practice
Event Invite

"Join us for our parish mission revival" speaks to the Core Active. "Come for dinner, stay for a conversation about what matters" speaks to the Lapsed and the Seeker. Same event. Different door.

Website Hero A homepage that opens with Mass times and the pastor's photo is built for people who already know you. One that opens with "Whether you're returning or just curious, you belong here" is built for people who don't yet.

Bulletin

A bulletin full of committee meeting schedules is a communication to insiders about insider business. One that leads with one meaningful invitation — specific, timely, with a reason to act — is communications.

Social Media

A post that announces a date and time reaches people who already care. A post that tells a story — a face, a moment, something real — reaches people who haven't decided yet.

What Matters

The Strategy Session

What a 90-Minute Session Looks Like

The session is facilitated by Roger and designed as a working session, not a lecture. The parish team should include the pastor, the person who manages the bulletin or communications, and anyone involved in ministry programming decisions.

0–12 min

Presence vs. Strategy — Reframing the Conversation

The Parishioner Journey. The honest distinction between having a website and having a communications strategy. Setting the frame for everything that follows.

12–30 min

Who Are Your People? — The Three-Dimension Audience Exercise

A structured conversation using the engagement, life stage, and faith experience framework. Who is this parish currently reaching? Who is it not reaching? Where is the growth opportunity — and is your communications aimed at it?

30–55 min

Your Audit Results — An Honest Assessment

Roger walks through findings from the pre-session audit: website, Google Analytics, bulletin, social, email. What's working. What isn't. Specific, constructive, and actionable — not a grade, a roadmap.

55–70 min

Brand and Voice — Does Your Communications Sound Like You?

Visual identity, tone of voice, photography. Does the parish's communications reflect who they actually are, or a generic institutional version of a parish? Practical examples of what the difference looks like.

70–85 min

Three Things — Written Before Anyone Leaves

Not a 50-point action plan. Three specific, realistic commitments — chosen together with the parish team — that will make a measurable difference in the next 30 days. Written down. Assigned to someone. With a follow-up date.

85–90 min

Introduction to the Training-ette Library

A brief walkthrough of the on-demand modules available on SetonAcademy — which ones are most relevant to this parish's situation and how to share them with staff and volunteers.

Your Website Situation

We Meet You Where You Are

No two parishes arrive in the same place digitally. Some have no real website at all. Some have one that hasn't been touched in years. Some are stuck in a WordPress installation that nobody on staff knows how to manage. Some have a solid foundation that just needs strategic direction. Seton's approach starts with an honest assessment of where the parish actually is — and a clear recommendation for the path forward.

1

No Website, or a Placeholder Page

Start from Zero
What We Find

A Facebook page serving as the parish's digital home. A GoDaddy "coming soon" page. A listing on a diocesan directory with no link. People searching Google and finding nothing — or finding a competitor parish instead.

What We Recommend
  • SetonDigital standard build — fast, clean, mobile-first
  • Custom domain on parish name
  • Google Analytics from day one
  • All compliance pages (Safe Environments, HR Reporting)
  • Mass times, contact, registration — clear and findable
What It Takes

One intake conversation with the pastor or administrator. Seton handles design, build, and deployment. Live within days, not months. No technical knowledge required from parish staff.

SetonDigital Standard build · Custom domain · Managed hosting · Analytics included
2

An Outdated Website Nobody Updates

Needs Rebuild
What We Find

A site built in 2014 that still lists the previous pastor. Events from three years ago. A photo of the church that looks nothing like it does today. Not mobile-friendly. No analytics. Nobody remembers who built it or where the login is.

What We Recommend
  • Full audit first — capture what's worth keeping
  • SetonDigital rebuild on clean foundation
  • Content migration and photography update
  • Google Analytics and Search Console setup
  • Bulletin pipeline connected to keep it current
What It Takes

A content conversation with a staff member who knows the parish. Roger reviews what exists. Seton rebuilds. The parish gets a site that reflects who they actually are today — and a workflow to keep it that way going forward.

SetonDigital Audit · Rebuild · Bulletin pipeline · Managed updates
3

A WordPress Site That's Become Unmanageable

Assess First
What We Find

A WordPress installation with 47 plugins, none of which anyone remembers installing. A theme purchased from a marketplace. Update warnings that have been ignored for two years. One volunteer who "handles the website" and is about to move away.

What We Recommend
  • Security and plugin audit before anything else
  • Decision: stabilize vs. rebuild based on findings
  • If WordPress is needed for dynamic features — stabilize and manage
  • If it's static content only — migrate to SetonDigital
  • Remove single-volunteer dependency either way
What It Takes

Honesty about what the parish actually needs from a website. Most parishes don't need WordPress — they need a fast, clean, always-updated site. The audit tells us which path is right. We never recommend a rebuild just because it's easier for us.

SetonTech + SetonDigital Security audit · Honest recommendation · Managed path forward
4

A Functional Site with No Strategy Behind It

Optimize
What We Find

A reasonably modern site that works fine technically. But the homepage is built for insiders. The Mass times page is buried. There's no call to action above the fold. Analytics shows most visitors leave in under 30 seconds. The site exists — it just isn't doing anything.

What We Recommend
  • Google Analytics deep review — find where people drop off
  • Homepage restructure around one primary action
  • Navigation simplification
  • Copy review — is it written for seekers or insiders?
  • Photography and visual identity update
What It Takes

Roger reviews the analytics and the site together. We identify the two or three changes that will have the highest impact — and make them. This is often the fastest win in the entire communications audit. A site people find but don't use is a solved problem.

SetonDigital + Roger Analytics review · Strategic restructure · Copy and visual refresh
5

A Bilingual or Multi-Cultural Parish

Specialized Build
What We Find

A parish serving Spanish, Portuguese, or other language communities — with a website built entirely in English. Or a site with a separate Spanish page that's six months out of date. The community that most needs to be reached is the one least served by what exists.

What We Recommend
  • Bilingual toggle built into the standard SetonDigital template
  • Both languages maintained in the bulletin pipeline equally
  • Community-specific photography and imagery
  • Language preference saved across visits
  • Mass times and contact info prominent in both languages
What It Takes

A content conversation with someone who knows both communities. Seton has built bilingual sites for parishes in the Diocese already — the template exists and works. The goal is a site where every parishioner sees their community reflected, not translated.

SetonDigital Bilingual ES/EN or PT/EN toggle · Equal content parity · Bulletin pipeline in both languages

Why This Is Worth the Effort

The Bottom Line

A beautiful website, a well-designed bulletin, and a strong social presence are not the goal. They are instruments. The goal is more people finding their way to the Church, and the people already there going deeper. Every communications decision should be tested against that standard and nothing else. This program exists to help parishes ask better questions — and build the habits that turn those questions into results.
